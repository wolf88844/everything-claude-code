# WeChat Content Principles

Do not treat a strong article as inspiration plus clean language.
Treat it as a repeatable sequence of writing decisions.

## When to Use

Use this skill when the user wants to:
- write a stronger WeChat article from the start
- understand why one article feels more powerful than another
- turn past writing wins into future writing rules
- improve openings, middle progression, endings, and quotable lines
- build a recognizable account voice instead of one-off decent drafts
- capture what makes an article worth reading, liking, following, and forwarding

## Core Principle

A strong WeChat article usually wins because it does five things well:
1. it stops the right reader on the first screen
2. it names a hidden discomfort more precisely than the reader can
3. it keeps deepening instead of repeating the same point
4. it controls paragraph rhythm instead of chopping every thought into isolated one-line fragments
5. it produces lines worth saving or sharing
6. it makes the account sound like it understands a larger survival problem

## Default Reader

Unless the user says otherwise, assume the article is for:
- ordinary office workers
- middle-career readers
- technical professionals with accumulated experience and vague growth anxiety

## What Strong Articles Usually Do

### 1. Lead With The Real Discomfort

Do not begin with event summary, concept summary, or moral summary.
Begin with the exact discomfort the reader feels but has not yet named well.

Good opening move:
- `It is not X. It is Y.`

Examples of stronger opening behavior:
- not `this news is trending`
- but `this news feels wrong for a deeper reason`

### 2. Move From Surface Reaction To Deeper Mechanism

The first reader reaction is rarely the real one.
Strong articles do not stop at:
- shock
- anger
- novelty
- fear of replacement

They ask:
- what is the deeper humiliation
- what is the deeper uncertainty
- what hidden mechanism does this reaction reveal

### 3. Build One Reusable Distinction

A memorable article usually builds one distinction it can keep returning to.

Examples:
- `did` vs `left behind`
- `familiarity` vs `asset`
- `busy` vs `moving forward`
- `self-understanding` vs `structural consequence`

If the article cannot be reduced to one reusable distinction, it is usually still too loose.

### 4. Progress By Reclassification, Not Repetition

Each section should deepen the diagnosis.
Do not keep restating the same feeling with different words.

A strong section usually does one of these:
- reframes the problem
- raises the cost
- sharpens the distinction
- moves from personal discomfort to structural consequence
- turns a scene into a mechanism

### 5. Produce Carrying Lines

A strong article needs lines that can travel outside the body.

Good carrying lines are:
- short
- contrastive
- precise
- emotionally exact

Examples of useful line shapes:
- `Doing a lot does not mean leaving a lot behind.`
- `Familiarity in role is not the same as an asset you can take away.`
- `The real problem is not replacement. It is realizing you never kept what you built.`

### 6. End Harder Than It Starts

Do not end by summarizing what the reader already knows.
End by tightening the consequence.

Weak ending:
- restates the theme

Stronger ending:
- asks what the reader has really left behind
- names what still does not belong to the reader
- points to the next uncomfortable but necessary action

## Writing Decision Rules

When shaping an article, prefer these moves:
- lead with the reader's unease, not the event chronology
- move from the obvious reading to the deeper reading
- create one sharp distinction early and keep returning to it
- turn lived scenes into mechanisms, not just observations
- keep mobile readability without sacrificing paragraph continuity and article-level flow
- explain without humiliating the reader
- close on a harder implication, not a softer summary

## Red Flags

The article is weakening if it does too much of this:
- starts with explanation instead of discomfort
- summarizes the event before naming why it matters
- uses many concepts but no strong distinction
- repeats the same diagnosis across sections
- breaks too many paragraphs into one-line fragments and loses article-level flow
- stays at self-understanding without pushing to structural cost
- ends with encouragement instead of consequence
- has no lines worth screenshotting

## Draft Review Checklist

Before finalizing, ask:
1. what is the real discomfort on the first screen
2. what is the deeper mechanism under that discomfort
3. what single distinction carries the article
4. where does the article get deeper instead of longer
5. which 3 lines are worth being quoted alone
6. what does the ending make newly unavoidable

## Output Format

When this skill is used explicitly, return:
1. strongest writing principles for this article
2. weakest current risks
3. 3 to 7 article-specific writing rules
4. recommended carry line shapes
5. ending pressure recommendation

## What To Capture Back Into The System

After a strong article or strong comparison, capture rules like:
- better first-screen move
- stronger distinction pattern
- stronger paragraph progression pattern
- more transferable carry line shape
- better ending move

The goal is not to praise one article.
The goal is to make future articles stronger before they are written.

## Constraints

- Do not reduce writing quality to abstract elegance.
- Do not reward clean concept writing if it lacks emotional truth.
- Do not confuse length with depth.
- Do not turn principles into formulaic copy.
- Do not end at taste; always convert insights into reusable writing rules.
