# WeChat Draft Comparison

Do not compare drafts by taste alone.
Compare them by reader action and reusable writing value.

## When to Use

Use this skill when the user wants to:
- choose between 2 or more article drafts
- understand which version is more likely to be read through, liked, followed, or shared
- extract the best parts from multiple drafts into one stronger version
- turn one comparison session into reusable rules for future公众号 writing
- compare drafts written around the same topic, title family, or mechanism

## Core Principle

The best draft is not the one with the cleanest concept.
It is the one that most clearly does all four:
- pulls the reader into the first screen
- names the hidden discomfort more sharply
- creates quotable lines worth saving or sharing
- makes the reader feel this account understands a deeper work-life problem

## Default Reader

Unless the user says otherwise, compare from the point of view of:
- ordinary office workers
- middle-career readers
- technical professionals with accumulated experience and vague career anxiety

## What To Judge

Always judge drafts across these dimensions:
1. hook strength
2. hidden-pain accuracy
3. progression force
4. quote line density
5. follow-worthiness
6. share-worthiness
7. alignment with the account's mother themes

## High-Value Signals

Prefer drafts that do more of these:
- reclassify the issue with a `not X, but Y` move
- turn a strange feeling into a precise mechanism
- distinguish `doing`, `familiarity`, and `asset`
- separate surface shock from deeper discomfort
- produce lines that can be quoted alone without losing force
- make the reader ask, `What have I actually left behind?`
- push from personal unease into structural consequence

## Common Winning Patterns

Look for these patterns when comparing:

### Pattern 1: Better Hook

A stronger draft often opens by correcting the obvious reading:
- weak: `this news is shocking`
- stronger: `it is not shocking because it looks human; it is shocking because it looks transferable`

### Pattern 2: Deeper Pain

A stronger draft does not stop at fear of replacement.
It exposes a more humiliating fear:
- maybe I did a lot, but left little behind

### Pattern 3: Sharper Distinction

Strong drafts create a contrast the reader can reuse:
- `did` vs `left behind`
- `familiarity` vs `asset`
- `still useful in role` vs `still valid outside role`

### Pattern 4: Better Propagation Lines

Strong drafts produce short lines that can travel:
- `Doing a lot does not mean leaving a lot behind.`
- `Skill in role is not the same as an asset you can take away.`

## Comparison Workflow

### Step 1: State the Winner

Choose a winner first.
Do not hide behind `both are good`.

### Step 2: Explain Why the Winner Wins

Use reader-action language:
- more likely to keep reading
- more likely to trigger a like
- more likely to earn a follow
- more likely to be forwarded to peers

### Step 3: Name What Each Weaker Draft Still Offers

Do not discard useful material.
Extract:
- best title line
- best paragraph
- best distinction
- best ending move

### Step 4: Distill Reusable Rules

Turn the comparison into 3 to 7 rules that future drafts can follow.

### Step 5: If Asked, Merge

When the user wants a final version:
- use the winner as the base
- pull only the strongest parts from the others
- do not create a stitched-together draft with duplicated moves

## Output Format

Default output should be:
1. winner
2. why it wins
3. what each weaker version still contributes
4. reusable writing rules
5. merge recommendation

## Rule Library To Capture

When distilling lessons, prefer rules like:
- lead with the reader's real discomfort, not the event summary
- turn the first reaction into a second, deeper realization
- build one memorable distinction the article can keep returning to
- use at least 3 quotable lines that survive extraction
- make the article sound like it reveals a larger survival problem, not just a hot take

## Constraints

- Do not choose a winner just because it is longer.
- Do not reward abstract correctness over emotional accuracy.
- Do not confuse more concepts with more force.
- Do not merge drafts by averaging them.
- Do not let the comparison end at preference; always produce reusable rules.
