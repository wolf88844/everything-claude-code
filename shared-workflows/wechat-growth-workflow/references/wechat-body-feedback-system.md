# WeChat Body Feedback System

Use this reference when article links or full article text are available.
This system upgrades feedback from title-level and metric-level learning into body-level learning.

## Goal

Do not stop at:
- this title worked
- this topic underperformed

Also learn:
- what kind of opening holds technical readers
- which diagnosis sections create forwarding desire
- where the article becomes abstract or loses tension
- which line or paragraph most likely carries the piece
- how a strong article moves from scene to diagnosis to lingering close

## What Counts As Body-Level Feedback

Body-level feedback means the article is reviewed as a full piece, not just by title and metrics.
Track these layers:
1. first-screen performance
2. opening scene quality
3. diagnosis depth
4. middle-section tension
5. quotable line density
6. ending pressure and carryover
7. technical-reader specificity
8. conversion from experience to judgment, expression, or asset

## When To Use

Use this system when at least one of these is available:
- article URL
- pasted article body
- full article document
- screenshot/archived copy of the article text

If only the title and metrics are available, fall back to `wechat-feedback-loop.md`.

## Body Review Template

For each reviewed article, record these sections.

### 1. Article identity
- title
- publish date
- content URL if available
- metrics snapshot: reads, shares, follows, read-finish rate
- topic family

### 2. First-screen diagnosis
Ask:
- does the first screen recreate a real work state?
- does it hit a technical worker's lived friction quickly?
- does it start too abstractly?
- does it create enough pressure to continue?

Classify the opening as one of these:
- scene-first
- emotion-first
- misunderstanding-first
- conclusion-first

### 3. Middle-body diagnosis
Check:
- where the article changes from scene into diagnosis
- whether the diagnosis gets deeper section by section
- whether the piece explains why the wrong explanation is attractive
- whether any paragraph starts sounding like framework dumping
- whether the technical worker specificity weakens in the middle

### 4. Carrying paragraph or carrying line
Identify:
- the one paragraph most likely carrying shares
- the one line most likely being screenshotted or quoted
- the section where the article becomes most recognizably this account's voice

### 5. Structural failure point
If the piece underperformed, identify the most likely body-level reason:
- opening too slow
- concept too early
- not enough work-scene specificity
- diagnosis not sharp enough
- middle collapsed into explanation
- ending too soft
- no quotable sentence

### 6. Reusable writing rule
Turn the article into one reusable rule.
Examples:
- technical readers keep reading when the opening recreates a meeting, review, or blame scene within the first screen
- articles about growth underperform when they do not anchor the concept in a real technical friction
- judgment/expression themes perform better when the middle contains one accountability conflict paragraph

## Body-Level Scorecard

Score each reviewed article from 1 to 5 on these dimensions:
1. first-screen hit
2. opening scene precision
3. diagnosis sharpness
4. technical specificity
5. middle-body tension
6. quotable line density
7. ending power
8. experience-to-asset conversion

Total: 40

Interpretation:
- 32-40: strong body-level model piece
- 24-31: useful sample with clear weak spots
- 16-23: structurally weak despite possible topic potential
- below 16: not a body-level reference sample

## Comparison Method

When several historical articles exist, compare them in pairs:
- high-read vs low-read on similar topic
- high-share vs low-share on similar topic
- high-follow vs high-read but low-follow

Compare especially:
- title promise vs body delivery
- first-screen difference
- where middle diagnosis becomes stronger or weaker
- whether the ending gives the reader a more shareable takeaway

## Priority Sampling Rule

When reviewing historical pieces, prioritize in this order:
1. high-share pieces
2. high-follow pieces
3. high-read pieces
4. representative underperformers in the same topic family

This helps identify not just what gets clicked, but what gets forwarded and what converts.

## Important Constraint

Do not try to learn every body-level rule from one article.
Use repeated structural signals across several articles before promoting them into the main writing rules.
Single-article insights should be labeled as tentative until repeated.

## System Output

A full body-level review should output:
1. metrics snapshot
2. article structure type
3. strongest body-level section
4. weakest body-level section
5. one reusable body-level rule
6. one variable to test in the next article

## Empirical Full-Sample Rules: 2026-03-05 to 2026-04-03

These rules come from a full-sample body-level comparison across 29 historical articles.
Promote them above single-article intuition unless newer data disproves them.

### What this account currently wins with
This account is strongest when it writes:
- a high-pressure technical work scene
- a hidden friction or invisible cost
- a quick problem reclassification
- a structural or organizational consequence

In short:
`technical scene + hidden friction + diagnosis + structural cost`

### Body-level rules now considered stable
1. Open with a technical pressure scene, not a broad growth mood.
2. Reclassify the problem early; do not stay too long in emotion explanation.
3. Push the article from personal discomfort into structural consequence.
4. Abstract growth topics must be attached to meetings, reviews, blame, rework, alignment failure, or decision scenes.
5. End by hardening the cost, not by soft encouragement.

### Repeated strong opening anchors
Repeatedly strong first-screen anchors include:
- review pressure
- blame or accountability scenes
- meeting drift or false consensus
- repeated re-questioning of past decisions
- execution layers absorbing undefined upstream work

### Repeated weak opening anchors
Repeatedly weaker opening anchors include:
- broad midlife anxiety
- broad career stagnation without a sharp work scene
- abstract growth or transition language in the first screen
- hotspot commentary without technical-worker daily friction

### Common strong middle-body move
A strong middle section for this account usually does this:
- names the reader's local pain
- redefines it sharply
- then expands it into a team, role, or organizational cost

### Common weak middle-body move
A weaker middle section often:
- explains the state accurately
- but stays in self-understanding too long
- and fails to convert the pain into a sharper structural diagnosis

## Additional Stable Rule: Story-To-Mechanism Upgrade

For a subset of articles, especially those about cognitive bias, self-justification, selective interpretation, or distorted hearing, a body-level winning structure can begin with a non-work story.

### When this structure is strong
It becomes strong when:
- the opening story is concrete, visual, and easy to retell
- the story contains the same mechanism that appears later in adult work life
- the bridge from story to adult diagnosis happens early enough
- the article does not stay in the story too long

### Strong body-level move
A strong version of this structure usually does:
1. a vivid small story creates instant understanding
2. the article names the hidden mechanism quickly
3. the article upgrades the mechanism into adult work, relationship, or growth behavior
4. the middle expands the cost of that bias
5. the ending hardens the cost into judgment, growth, or collaboration damage

### Failure mode to watch
This structure becomes weak when:
- the story is charming but not diagnostic
- the article spends too long in the anecdote
- the bridge into adult relevance is late or soft
- the deeper cost is not made explicit

### Practical comparison rule
If two drafts exist for the same topic:
- one opens with workplace mechanism
- one opens with a vivid story that explains the mechanism first

Prefer the story-led version when it improves:
- first-screen stopping power
- memory of the central mechanism
- ease of forwarding to a broader audience

## Additional Stable Rule: Restart-Ritual To Mechanism Upgrade

For articles about repeated restarting, wanting to change, or fake progress, a strong body-level structure usually does not begin with a general lecture about discipline.

### When this structure is strong
It becomes strong when:
- the opening uses a restart ritual the reader has genuinely done
- the article names the emotional repair or relief in that ritual
- the middle distinguishes activation from accumulation
- the article explains why real change feels much less rewarding in the short term
- the ending hardens the cost: false motion, restart addiction, or no real accumulation

### Strong body-level move
A strong version usually does:
1. small restart ritual
2. emotional payoff of that ritual
3. diagnosis: this is change-feeling, not change
4. contrast: real change is slower and less exciting
5. cost: repeated restarting without durable accumulation

### Failure mode to watch
This structure becomes weak when:
- the article says "people always restart" but never shows the ritual vividly
- the article becomes generic self-help or anti-procrastination commentary
- the contrast between activation and accumulation is not made concrete
- the ending does not clarify what the reader is actually losing

### Practical comparison rule
If two drafts exist for the same topic:
- one mainly states the diagnosis
- one reconstructs the restart ritual, the emotional reward, and the false-progress loop

Prefer the loop-reconstruction version when it improves:
- recognition
- forwarding potential
- reader self-identification
- memorability of the mechanism

## Additional Stable Rule: Grievance-To-Mechanism Upgrade

For workplace grievance topics, a body-level winning structure often depends on upgrading one uncomfortable event into an explanation of organizational reality.

### When this structure is strong
It becomes strong when:
- the opening grievance is concrete and familiar
- the article names the emotional discomfort precisely
- the article quickly shows that the event is not isolated
- the middle upgrades the grievance into boundary, default order, interpretation power, atmosphere, or organizational control
- the ending hardens the cost into a durable truth about how ordinary employees keep losing in that system

### Strong body-level move
A strong version usually does:
1. one small workplace hurt creates recognition
2. the article names why the moment feels so awkward or unfair
3. the article widens into a larger pattern
4. the pattern is renamed as organizational mechanism or default order
5. the ending leaves the reader with a clearer model of how the company actually runs

### Failure mode to watch
This structure becomes weak when:
- the article stays at the level of personal grievance too long
- the incident is vivid but not upgraded into a broader pattern
- the middle remains emotional but not diagnostic
- the ending does not widen the event into organizational consequence

### Practical comparison rule
If two drafts exist for the same topic:
- one stays close to the specific grievance
- one upgrades the grievance into a broader explanation of company reality

Prefer the broader mechanism version when it improves:
- cross-reader recognition beyond the original event
- quotable lines about how organizations actually work
- forwarding value because the article explains a hidden workplace rule


## Additional Stable Rule: Structural-Stall Explanation Beats Early Advice In Execution-Layer Articles

When comparing two versions of an article about why an execution-layer reader is not moving up, prefer the version that first explains the stall itself rather than the version that quickly moves to advice.

A stronger version usually:
- recreates the feeling of doing more and becoming more reliable without position change
- explains why execution value does not automatically turn into upward movement
- distinguishes execution value from judgment value
- only then lands on the upgrade path

A version that jumps early to "you need to learn to speak clearly" may be correct, but will often feel more generic and less penetrating.

## Additional Stable Rule: Pain-First Beats Mechanism-First In Spread-Oriented Institution Articles

When comparing two versions of a??/??/???? article, do not automatically prefer the version that explains the mechanism more completely.

If the reader role is a broad office worker or middle-career employee and the content's goal includes spread, prefer the version that:
- recreates the concrete moment of being lightly pushed back
- preserves the emotional discomfort of "I followed the rules and still got pressed back"
- only moves into organizational mechanism after the reader has fully recognized the scene

A more complete explanation can still lose if it flattens the lived sting and turns the article into a smoother but less memorable diagnosis.

## Additional Stable Rule: Rule-Failure To Organization-Priority Upgrade

For articles about?????????????, a stronger body often does more than prove that written rules are unstable.
It upgrades the article by explaining what the organization protects when written rules begin to bend.

A stronger version usually contains:
1. a recognizable everyday scene where the rule appears valid
2. the turning point where the rule becomes a ?special case?
3. a naming of the organizational priority behind the softness: stability, convenience, order, face, or preserved allocation
4. a closing judgment that the reader can reuse beyond the original case
