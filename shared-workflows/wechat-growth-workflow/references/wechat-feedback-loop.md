# WeChat Feedback Loop

Use this reference to create an active feedback mechanism for公众号 writing.
The purpose is not to wait passively for vague outcomes. The purpose is to form a testable loop before publishing and a reusable learning loop after publishing.

## Core Principle

Do not ask only: "Was this article good?"
Ask instead:
- what was this article trying to win on?
- which signal would tell us it actually worked?
- what should be kept, changed, or tested next time?

## Before Publishing: Propagation Hypothesis

Before finalizing an article, state a short hypothesis with these fields:

1. `Primary spread driver`
Choose one main driver:
- recognition of a lived state
- a sharp diagnosis
- a surprising reframe
- a screenshot-worthy sentence
- a strong title curiosity gap

2. `Reader response we expect`
Examples:
- "this is exactly my current state"
- "I never thought about it this way"
- "this explains why I keep getting stuck"
- "I want to send this to a friend or colleague"

3. `Most important section`
Which part must work for the article to spread?
- title
- opening
- core diagnosis section
- ending question

4. `Failure risk`
What is the most likely reason the article may underperform?
Examples:
- too abstract too early
- not technical-worker-specific enough
- strong idea but weak first screen
- emotionally accurate but not quotable enough

## After Publishing: Active Feedback Capture

After publishing, collect feedback in four layers whenever signals are available.

### Layer 1: Surface performance
Capture what can be observed externally.
Examples:
- relative reading performance versus recent posts
- saves, shares, comments, likes, follows if available
- whether the title appears to have pulled people in

### Layer 2: Reader language
Capture audience wording, not just metrics.
Examples:
- comments that say "this is me"
- comments that repeat a specific line
- messages that reveal what part landed hardest
- confusion signals that show where the article was unclear

### Layer 3: Spread trigger
Identify what actually carried the piece.
Possible triggers:
- title did most of the work
- opening created recognition
- one line was highly screenshot-worthy
- middle diagnosis caused people to share it
- the ending question triggered discussion

### Layer 4: Reusable learning
Turn the result into a reusable rule.
Examples:
- "for this audience, second-growth topics spread better when the wrong path is shown first"
- "technical readers respond more when the opening includes concrete work scenes"
- "abstract concept headlines underperform compared with diagnosis headlines"

## Minimal Post-Publish Review

After each published piece, produce a short review with:
1. what the article tried to win on
2. what signal suggests it worked or failed
3. what part most likely carried the article
4. one thing to keep next time
5. one thing to test next time

## Important Constraint

Do not wait for massive data before learning.
Even a few comments, replies, repost choices, or message patterns can generate a useful rule.
Small signals should still be captured and turned into testable next-step hypotheses.

## System Role

This feedback loop should serve both of these workflows:
- `wechat-growth-workflow` for writing and evaluation
- `social-inspiration-workflow` for future topic and packaging selection

## Historical Baseline: 2026-03-05 to 2026-04-03

Use this dataset as the current empirical baseline unless newer data replaces it.
Source summary:
- 29 articles
- average reads: 1567.72
- median reads: 77
- average shares: 66.59
- average follows: 14.07

### Stronger-performing topic patterns
- technical meetings, re-questioning, review pressure, and hidden team frictions
- titles that name a specific technical work scene instead of a broad growth concept
- articles that expose a hidden cost in day-to-day execution or communication
- expression and judgment topics when framed as conflict, accountability, or misunderstanding

### Weaker-performing topic patterns
- broad 35-plus anxiety topics without a sharp technical work scene
- hotspot borrowing that does not connect tightly to the reader's daily technical reality
- abstract growth, experience, or transition ideas that are not translated into a concrete friction

### Current empirical rules
1. For this account, `technical scene + hidden friction` is stronger than `abstract growth concept`.
2. Titles about meetings, re-questioning, blame, or stalled collaboration are currently the clearest spread drivers.
3. `很多技术人...` works better when it is followed by a concealed friction or misdiagnosis, not a generic self-improvement promise.
4. Hot topics should usually be translated into a technical worker's daily dilemma before entering the main writing workflow.
5. Judgment and expression themes can perform well, but they need a sharper scene, conflict, or accountability frame.

### Baseline examples to remember
- breakout sample: `技术人为什么会对“复盘式追问”特别敏感？`
- strong managerial pressure sample: `为什么越专业的技术领导，越容易把团队带窒息`
- strong expression/judgment sample: `很多技术人被问责，不是因为事情做错了，而是因为他不会把事实说成判断`
- weak pattern sample: hotspot-derived commentary without direct daily technical friction

## Upgrade Path: Body-Level Feedback

When article URLs or full article text are available, do not stop at title-level feedback.
Upgrade into body-level review using `wechat-body-feedback-system.md`.

Body-level feedback should answer:
- what kind of first screen this account actually wins with
- which paragraphs are carrying shares or follows
- where strong topics become too abstract in the middle
- what kind of ending helps technical readers carry the piece forward

Promote a rule into the main system only after the same body-level signal appears repeatedly across multiple articles.

## Full-Sample Body-Level Upgrade: 2026-03-05 to 2026-04-03

After reviewing the full historical sample, the most reliable spread pattern is now clearer:
- the article should open inside a technical work scene
- the article should expose a hidden friction or invisible cost
- the middle should rename the problem fast
- the article should not stop at emotion; it should escalate into structural consequence
- the ending should land on a harder cost sentence

### What to test forward
When drafting future pieces, prefer testing variables inside this winning structure rather than changing everything at once.
Good variables to test:
- how concrete the opening scene is
- how quickly the diagnosis is renamed
- how hard the ending cost sentence lands

### What to avoid repeating
- broad recognition pieces without work-scene tension
- concept-first growth articles
- hotspot borrowing that does not convert into a technical worker's daily dilemma

## New Empirical Rule: Story-Led Mechanism Articles

A new pattern is now validated:
For articles about bias, self-serving interpretation, selective hearing, self-justification, and judgment distortion, a story-led opening can outperform a direct workplace-mechanism opening.

### Why it worked
The stronger version did not begin by analyzing team collaboration.
It began with a vivid child scene that made the mechanism instantly understandable:
- wanting a result first
- then searching for an explanation that permits that result

This improved:
- stop rate on the first screen
- memory of the article's core idea
- shareability beyond one narrow workplace context

### New reusable rule
When the article's real subject is a broad human cognitive move, test whether a small vivid story can explain the mechanism faster than a direct work scene.
If yes, prefer:
1. story first
2. mechanism second
3. adult/workplace translation third
4. structural cost last

### What to look for after publishing
For story-led mechanism articles, observe whether readers respond with:
- "一下就懂了"
- "这不只是小孩，成年人也一样"
- quoting the story itself when summarizing the article
- forwarding because the story is easy to retell

If readers mostly discuss the opening story itself but do not carry forward the deeper diagnosis, the bridge from story to adult mechanism is too weak.
If readers repeat the story and the deeper judgment together, the article structure is working.

## New Empirical Rule: Restart-Loop And Change-Feeling Articles

A new pattern is now validated:
For articles about restarting, wanting to change, repeated planning, and self-renewal rituals, a stronger version often explains the psychological reward loop before stating the conclusion.

### Why it worked
The stronger version did not only say that people like the feeling of change.
It reconstructed the loop:
- a concrete restart ritual
- an immediate emotional release
- the relief of feeling morally repaired
- the later absence of real accumulation

This improved:
- first-screen recognition
- reading depth
- shareability, because readers felt not only judged but accurately understood

### New reusable rule
When the article is about repeated restarting or fake progress, test whether the piece clearly explains:
1. what action creates the restart feeling
2. why that feeling is rewarding
3. how that reward substitutes for actual growth
4. what real change feels like by contrast

### What to look for after publishing
For restart-loop articles, observe whether readers respond with:
- "这就是我每次立计划的状态"
- "原来我不是在改变，是在获得一种开始的感觉"
- quoting lines about restart rituals, activation, or change-feeling
- forwarding because the article describes a loop they keep repeating

If readers mostly agree with the conclusion but do not talk about the loop itself, the mechanism was not reconstructed deeply enough.
If readers repeat both the ritual and the diagnosis, the structure is working.

## New Empirical Rule: Grievance-To-Organization Articles

A new pattern is now validated:
When an article starts from one concrete workplace grievance, the stronger version often does not stay at the level of hurt.
It upgrades the event into a clearer explanation of how the organization actually runs.

### Why it worked
The stronger version widened the entry point.
It did not only say "this one thing felt wrong".
It explained:
- why explicit rules and real boundaries diverge
- how default order overrides written process
- how one phrase can reveal an entire organizational logic

This improved:
- read-through, because more readers could map their own situations onto the article
- saves and forwarding potential, because the article became a framework, not just a complaint
- author credibility, because the piece felt like organizational diagnosis rather than venting

### New reusable rule
When the source material is a specific workplace grievance, test whether the article becomes stronger if it moves through this sequence:
1. one concrete hurt or awkward moment
2. the precise discomfort inside that moment
3. the hidden rule or hidden signal behind it
4. the broader organizational mechanism
5. the structural cost to ordinary employees or execution layers

### What to look for after publishing
For grievance-to-organization articles, observe whether readers respond with:
- "公司就是这么运转的"
- "制度写的是一套，现实跑的是另一套"
- forwarding because the article explains a familiar but hard-to-name workplace truth
- quoting lines about boundary, atmosphere, default order, or interpretation power

If readers mostly sympathize with the incident but do not repeat the broader mechanism, the upgrade is too weak.
If readers retell the event as proof of a larger organizational reality, the structure is working.


## Success Spec Continuity Rule

Every article should be reviewed against the same success target it was assigned before drafting.

Minimum continuity check:
1. What was the primary goal: recognition, spread, relationship, or conversion?
2. Did the predicted minimum success signal appear?
3. Did the likely failure mode appear instead?
4. Was the article incorrectly judged because the wrong metric was used?

Do not review a recognition piece as if it were only a spread piece.
Do not review a bridge or trust-building piece as if it needed breakout traffic to be useful.
